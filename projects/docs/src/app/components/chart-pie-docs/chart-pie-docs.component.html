When a pie is the right shape

A pie answers exactly one question well: what share of a whole is each part? It is a poor instrument for anything else. Comparing two slices of similar size is harder than comparing two bars, and comparing across two pies is harder still, because the reader has to judge angles rather than lengths.

Use it when the parts genuinely sum to a meaningful total and there are few enough of them to name. Reach for <tolle-chart> with bars when you are ranking things, and with a line when you are showing change over time.

Parts of one whole

Traffic by source, revenue by plan, storage by bucket. Values are positive and the total means something.

Not a ranking, not a trend

Twelve near-equal slices, or two pies side by side asking “did this grow?”. Both are questions a reader cannot answer from angles.

Installation

Add it with the CLI (you own the source), or import the component if you use the package.

Anatomy

Unlike <tolle-chart>, the pie has no projected parts. It is a single element, and the legend, the hover tooltip and the accessible data table are rendered by the component itself — you never add them as children.

Rows are plain objects. Point labelKey and valueKey at whichever properties hold the name and the number:

Slices are separated by a real angular gap rather than a stroke, so what divides them is surface showing through rather than added ink. A sliver never disappears into the gap — it keeps a hairline of itself.

Donut

Set [donut]="true" to cut a hole, and innerRadius to size it. The hole is not decoration — it is the one place on a pie where a total can sit without competing with the slices, so put the number the parts add up to there.

Many slices and the palette limit

The palette has five steps, --chart-1 through --chart-5, validated together for lightness, chroma and colour-blind separation. A sixth slice does not get a generated colour: it renders in a neutral grey, because inventing a step would land outside that validation and wrapping back to step 1 would give two slices the same identity.

Colour is assigned by slice label, not by array position. Hide a region below and the remaining slices keep exactly the colour they had — re-sorting or filtering never repaints the survivors, which is what lets a reader carry a colour between this chart and the next.

Accessibility

A pie encodes every value in an angle and an identity in a colour, so on its own it is unreadable to anyone who cannot see it or cannot hover it. Three things carry the data instead, and all three are automatic:

  • The legend, rendered whenever there are two or more slices, so identity is never carried by colour alone.
  • A real <table> of every label and value. It is sr-only by default and always pointed at by the svg's aria-describedby, so the numbers stay reachable without seeing or hovering the chart. Set [showTable]="true" to show it to everyone.
  • ariaLabel becomes the svg <title> and description the <desc>. Write the description as the sentence you would say out loud about the chart, not as a restatement of its title.

Name the table's columns with labelHeader and valueHeader — the defaults are “Category” and “Value”, which tell a screen-reader user nothing.
