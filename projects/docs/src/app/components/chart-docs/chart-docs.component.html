The problem

Most chart libraries hand you a picture. A picture is the one part of a chart that some of your readers will never get: colour-blind readers cannot separate the series, screen-reader users get an <svg> with nothing in it, and anyone on a touch device cannot hover for the value.

<tolle-chart> treats those as part of the chart, not as an add-on. The container renders the plot, and alongside it a legend and a real <table> of the numbers — always, without you asking. You compose the marks; you do not have to remember the rest.

Shared scales

Every mark draws into one coordinate space, owned by a per-chart service. Adding a bar switches the whole chart to a band scale; adding an area forces zero into the domain.

Colour by identity

A series keeps its palette step for the life of the chart, keyed by its key. Filtering one out never repaints the others.

Readable without the picture

The legend and the sr-only table are rendered by the container, so the values survive colour blindness, a screen reader and a device with no pointer.

Installation

Add it with the CLI (you own the source), or import the parts if you use the package. Import only the marks you actually draw.

Anatomy

Read this first: the marks are attribute directives on svg:g

Every part is written as <svg:g tolle-chart-line seriesKey="revenue">, never as <tolle-chart-line>.

This is not a style preference. A custom element is created in the HTML namespace, and an HTML element inside an <svg> never renders its SVG subtree. Get it wrong and you get a blank chart with no error — no console warning, no template failure, just an empty frame.

The second rule is what you do not put inside. The legend, the hover tooltip and the accessible table are rendered by the container itself. They are not projected content — adding them as children gives you two of each.

Data and series

data is an array of plain rows in x order. xKey names the property holding the category label, and each ChartSeries names a property to read as a value:

A missing or non-numeric value is a gap, not a zero — the line skips it rather than diving to the baseline and inventing a collapse that never happened. Y ticks are snapped to the next 1 / 2 / 5 × 10n step, so the axis reads 0 / 10 / 20 rather than 0 / 19.4 / 38.8.

One y scale, deliberately

There is no secondary y-axis, and this is a decision rather than a gap. Two measures on two scales in one frame let the author choose where the lines cross, so the reader sees a relationship that the data does not contain. Shift one axis by a factor and a correlation appears; shift it back and it is gone.

When you need to compare two things measured in different units, use two charts stacked as small multiples, or index both series to a common base (say, 100 at the first period) and plot them on one honest scale.

Examples

Line

Two series over time. Hover anywhere in a column — the hit area is the whole band, so you aim at a month, not at a 2px stroke — and the tooltip lists every series at that x at once.

curve="smooth" is monotone cubic, not a Catmull-Rom spline: it is constrained never to overshoot the data, so the curve cannot bulge past a measured point and imply a peak that was never recorded.

Bars, grouped and stacked

Adding a tolle-chart-bar switches the whole chart to a band scale and forces zero into the y domain — a bar encodes its value as a length, so a truncated baseline would misstate every comparison on the chart.

stacked lives on the container, not on the mark, because stacking is a property of the frame that all the marks share. Stacked answers “how big is the total, and how is it composed?”; grouped answers “how do these compare within each period?”. Toggle between them below.

Bars, horizontal

orientation="horizontal" transposes the whole plot: the category axis runs down the left and the value axis runs along the bottom. Only bars support it — line and area marks stay vertical regardless of what the container is set to.

Widen the left margin to fit the category labels; they take the space the bottom axis used to need.

Combo

A bar and a line are just two marks projected into the same tolle-chart — nothing about combining them is special. What makes it honest is sharing the one y scale: revenue and target are the same unit here, so overlaying them actually compares them rather than implying a relationship the axes engineered.

Area

An area is a line with a low-opacity wash down to its baseline, so it also pulls zero into the domain. Stacked, each series sits on the top of the one below and the whole shape reads as a total.

This one sets [showTable]="true" so you can see the fallback that is normally sr-only. It is rendered on every chart whether or not you show it.

Colour

Series take the theme's chart steps, --chart-1 through --chart-5. The dark-mode steps are chosen independently rather than derived by lightening the light ones, because a mechanical flip wrecks the separation the palette was picked for.

Assigned by key, not by position

A key claims a slot the first time it is seen and keeps it. Filter a series out of the array and back in, or drop an earlier one, and the survivors do not move a step — so a colour a reader has learnt stays attached to the same thing.

Five steps, then a neutral

A sixth series does not get a generated colour — it renders in --muted-foreground. Generating a step would land outside the palette's validation, and wrapping back to --chart-1 would give two series one identity. Both are worse than being visibly uncoloured.

If you have more than five things to plot, that is a signal about the chart rather than about the palette: group the tail into an “Other” series, or split into small multiples.

Accessibility

The palette is optimised for hue separation across the common forms of colour blindness, and one of its five steps sits below 3:1 contrast against the surface. That is a deliberate trade — pushing every step to 3:1 would collapse the hues toward each other and make the series harder to tell apart, which is the failure the palette exists to avoid.

The trade only holds because colour is never the sole carrier of meaning. Two things guarantee that, and the container renders both for you:

  • The legend, rendered automatically whenever there are two or more series, so every series is named as well as coloured. A single series gets no legend, because the chart's own label already names it.
  • The table fallback: a real <table> of every x position and every series value, sr-only by default and always pointed at by the svg's aria-describedby. Anything the chart encodes in colour or position stays reachable without seeing it and without hovering it.

Two inputs are on you. ariaLabel becomes the svg <title> and is the chart's accessible name. description becomes the <desc> — write the sentence you would say to someone looking over your shoulder, the finding rather than a restatement of the title. Set xHeader too; the default “Category” tells a screen-reader user nothing about what the rows are.

Hover targets are sized for people, not for pixels: the pointer catchment for each x position is the whole band, so a reader aims at a category rather than at a 2px line or an 8px dot. Dots are 8px across at the default radius for the same reason.
